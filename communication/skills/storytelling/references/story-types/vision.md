# Story Type: Future Product Vision / North Star

## When to use
- Present the long-term direction of the product
- Align team/stakeholders around a common objective
- Opening a planning cycle (OKRs, annual roadmap)
- "What will we be in X years?"

## Framework: Future-Back Narrative

Starts at the desired future and works back to the present. Data serves as an anchor of reality, not as a starting point.

### Required structure

```
1. IMAGINE — paint the future in active voice, present tense
   "It's 2028. The operations manager opens the dashboard and sees..."
   → Concrete, visual, specific. Not "we will be leaders", but "Jane does X in 30 seconds"

2. WHAT NEEDS TO BE TRUE — the conditions that make that future real
   → 3–5 testable premises: user behavior, technical capability, market
   → Each premise is an explicit bet

3. WHERE WE ARE TODAY — honest gap between future and present
   → Current data as proof of the gap, not as a starting point
   → "Today, the same flow takes 4 hours and 3 screens"

4. WHY NOW — urgency and window of opportunity
   → Market, behavior, competition, regulation change
   → What makes this the right moment (and what makes waiting risky)

5. NEXT STEPS — what we need to start doing
   → Max 3 concrete actions with owner and deadline
```

## Skills to invoke

| When | Skill |
|---|---|
| Jobs-to-be-Done in the future | `jtbd` — map the job we want to serve at scale |
| Customer voice about current pain | `synthesize-research` — what customers say about the problem today |
| Real customer quotes | `research-feedback` — verify FEEDBACK-LOG before any citation |
| Decision about which vision to pursue | `product-decision` — when there are multiple possible directions |
| Market context data | `data-research` |

## Non-negotiable rules

- **Concrete, not abstract.** "The manager does X" > "We will empower managers"
- **One vision, one sentence.** If it doesn't fit in 1 sentence, it's not a vision yet — it's a wish list
- **Real customer voice.** If there's no real customer quote, mark `[NO CUSTOMER VOICE]` and collect before presenting
- **Honest gap.** Don't minimize the size of the change — audiences detect forced optimism as lack of credibility
- **Explicit bets.** Every premise about the future is a bet — naming the risks shows maturity

## Collection questions (one at a time)

1. "What time horizon are we talking about? (1 year / 3 years / 5+ years)"
2. "Who is the main user in this vision? Who is the protagonist of the story?"
3. "What is the main pain/job today that the vision solves at scale?"
4. "Do you have a customer quote that captures the current problem?"
5. "What are the 2–3 bets we need to make to get there?"

## Recommended output

| Audience | Preferred format |
|---|---|
| Board / C-level | `exec-memo` (1 page) + 3 supporting slides (`deck-outline`) |
| Squad / internal team | Full `deck-outline` with debate on bet points |
| All-hands | `deck-outline` with strong emotional opening (customer story) |

## Anti-patterns

- Vision as a feature list ("we will have X, Y, Z") — not a vision, it's a backlog
- Future without tension — vision without difficulty is not inspiring, it's generic
- Data as a starting point — start with the future, use data to show the gap
- "Pillars" or "circular strategy" slide without narrative — audience forgets in 24h
