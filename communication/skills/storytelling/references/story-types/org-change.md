# Story Type: Org Structure / Reorgs

## When to use
- Announce a team or squad structure change
- Redistribute responsibilities between areas
- Create or dissolve a group
- Change in reporting or mission
- "How we're reorganizing to achieve X"

## Framework: Change Narrative (ADKAR adapted)

Reorgs are decided with logic but experienced with emotion. The argument structure matters less than the order in which you address fear. Credibility here comes from honesty about what will change AND what won't.

### The fears the audience brings (always, even without verbalizing)

- "Will my role disappear?"
- "Will I have to work with someone difficult?"
- "Who decides what now?"
- "Why are we changing again?"
- "Is this about me specifically?"

The structure below was designed to answer these fears in the right order.

### Required structure (ADKAR adapted)

```
1. AWARENESS — why change now
   → The problem the current structure creates (concrete, not abstract)
   → "Today the decision flow on X goes through 3 teams, which creates..."
   → Don't attack people — attack the system

2. DESIRE — what we gain from the change
   → Concrete benefit for the TEAM, not just the company
   → "With this change, decisions on Y will take hours, not weeks"
   → Connect to the daily work of whoever is listening

3. WHAT DOESN'T CHANGE (crucial — frequently omitted)
   → Product mission? Unchanged.
   → People? Everyone stays.
   → Values and ways of working? Unchanged.
   → Audience needs to hear this explicitly — don't assume it's obvious

4. KNOWLEDGE — what exactly changes
   → New structure drawn (visual required)
   → Who reports to whom
   → Who decides what (RACI or simplified equivalent)
   → Transition timeline

5. ABILITY — how we'll support the transition
   → Support available: 1:1s, docs, Q&A sessions
   → Point of contact for questions
   → What happens in the next 30 days

6. REINFORCEMENT — how we'll know it worked
   → Success metric for the change (not product — team functioning)
   → When we review whether it's working
```

## Skills to invoke

| When | Skill |
|---|---|
| There's data on friction in the current structure | `data-research` |
| Decision between structure models | `decision-making` — Cynefin to evaluate complexity |
| Impact communication to external stakeholders | prepare update using `audience-adaptation` framing |

## Non-negotiable rules

- **Name the fears before the audience shouts them.** "I know the first question is X" — this builds trust
- **What doesn't change comes BEFORE what changes.** Audience won't absorb change if still in defense mode
- **Never frame the previous structure as "wrong."** It was right for the moment — the context changed
- **There's no "perfect" reorg.** Every structure optimizes some flows and worsens others — be honest about trade-offs
- **Written FAQ available.** After the announcement, questions arise in private — have a Q&A document ready

## Collection questions

1. "What concrete problem does the current structure create? (process, decision, duplication)"
2. "What explicitly WON'T change? (people, mission, product)"
3. "What is the transition timeline?"
4. "What are the hardest questions you expect to receive?"
5. "Who needs to be informed before the broad announcement?"

## Recommended output

| Audience | Preferred format |
|---|---|
| All-hands / full team | `deck-outline` with structure visual + Q&A moment |
| Leadership / C-level (pre-alignment) | Concise `exec-memo` |
| Affected individuals | `verbal-brief` — 1:1 conversation before the broad announcement |

## Anti-patterns

- Announcing change without explaining the problem it solves
- Presenting new structure without showing what doesn't change
- Improvised FAQ — hard questions answered on the spot with inconsistency
- "We're excited about this change!" without acknowledging the real disruption
- Using a reorg to address a specific individual's performance issue — audience notices
