# Framework: Persuasion (Ethos-Pathos-Logos)

## When to use

When the goal is not to inform but to move — to get someone to approve, prioritize, fund, or change their mind. Every presentation has persuasive elements; this framework is for when persuasion IS the objective.

## The three pillars (Aristotle — still works)

### Ethos — Credibility

"Why should I listen to you about this?"

Built by:
- Data with verifiable source
- Acknowledging the limitations of your own argument
- Track record of previous decisions that worked
- Real customer voice (not paraphrase)
- Admitting what you don't know

**How to use in the story:** establish credibility in the first 60 seconds. Not with credentials — with precision and honesty about the problem.

**Anti-pattern:** claiming authority without demonstrating it ("trust me, it's the best solution")

---

### Pathos — Emotional Connection

"Why does this matter to me?"

Built by:
- Specific story of a real customer (name, context, impact)
- Human consequence of not solving the problem
- Concrete vision of how things look after the solution
- Before/after contrast with a real person

**How to use in the story:** usually in the opening (to create emotional tension) and in the closing (to resolve with a positive vision). Data goes in the middle.

**How to collect:** verify your feedback/research log before any story. If no real quote is available: `[NO CUSTOMER VOICE — collect before presenting]`

**Anti-pattern:** fabricated pathos ("imagine a user who...") — the audience notices and loses trust

---

### Logos — Logical Argument

"What is the evidence that this is true?"

Built by:
- Quantitative data with context (n=, period, source)
- Analysis of alternatives with explicit criteria
- Causal connection between problem and solution
- Projection with declared assumptions

**How to use in the story:** supports the argument after pathos creates engagement. Logos without pathos does not move; pathos without logos does not convince.

**Anti-pattern:** logos as theater (citing numbers with no connection to the recommendation)

---

## Complete persuasive story structure

```
OPENING — Pathos
  Specific customer story that illustrates the problem
  (30-60 seconds — concrete, visual, specific)

PROBLEM — Logos
  Data showing the problem is real and significant
  "This is not an isolated case — X% of customers face this"

SOLUTION — Logos + Ethos
  How we solve it, why it works, evidence
  Alternatives considered and why this is better

EXPECTED IMPACT — Logos + Pathos
  Success metric + what the user's life looks like after

CALL TO ACTION — clear, specific, with deadline
  "We need [approval/resource/decision] by [date] to [reason]"

CLOSING — Pathos
  Return to the opening story with the resolution
  "The user before waited 4h. With this, it takes 30 min."
```

## Call to Action — must be specific

| Vague (avoid) | Specific (use) |
|---|---|
| "We need to move forward with this" | "We need approval to include in the April sprint" |
| "It would be good to have more resources" | "We need 1 additional engineer for 6 weeks" |
| "Let's think about this together" | "I propose we decide by Friday — I can send the memo today" |

## Anticipating objections

Before presenting, list the 3 most likely objections:
1. "We don't have budget/resources for this"
2. "This is not a priority right now"
3. "What if [specific risk] happens?"

For each objection: have a prepared answer OR openly acknowledge it as a legitimate concern and show how you think about it.

## Anti-patterns

- Pathos without logos: "it needs to change because it's frustrating for the user" — that's not an argument
- Logos without pathos: slide full of data without a story — doesn't move anyone
- Implicit call to action — audience left without knowing what you needed from them
- Pretending there are no objections — audience loses trust when you don't acknowledge real difficulties
