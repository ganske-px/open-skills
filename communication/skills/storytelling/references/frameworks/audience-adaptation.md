# Framework: Audience Adaptation

## Principle

The same story is told differently to different audiences. The content may be the same — what changes is: what comes first, the level of detail, the tone, and what you leave out.

## Audience profiles

### Exec / C-level / Leadership

**What matters:** business impact, decision speed, managed risk
**What does NOT matter:** implementation details, analytical journey, context they already have

**Rules:**
- Recommendation/conclusion ALWAYS first
- Max 3 supporting points
- Numbers with unit and context ("18% less than last month", not "18%")
- Presentation time: 10 min, decision in 5
- Questions: anticipate the 3 most likely and have answers ready

**Attention triggers:** "this impacts our primary metric because...", "the risk of not acting is..."

---

### Engineering / Technical team

**What matters:** technical accuracy, real trade-offs, honesty about uncertainties
**What does NOT matter:** emotional narrative, excessive business context

**Rules:**
- Explicit trade-offs — do not present a "perfect solution"
- Name what you don't know before they ask
- Data and sources available
- Room for technical debate — not a one-way presentation
- Estimates with uncertainty range, not false precise numbers

**Attention triggers:** "here are the trade-offs we found...", "what we still don't know is..."

---

### Squad / Product team

**What matters:** mission clarity, impact on their work, room to contribute
**What does NOT matter:** exec-level detail, excessive strategic abstraction

**Rules:**
- Connect to the user's job-to-be-done, not the corporate OKR
- Show how the team's work connects to the larger objective
- Leave room for questions and adjustments
- Focus on "what we do now" more than "where we're going in 3 years"

**Attention triggers:** "what this means for your work is...", "I need your input on..."

---

### Customers / End users

**What matters:** concrete benefits, real use cases, proof that you understand their problem
**What does NOT matter:** internal details, business metrics, product jargon

**Rules:**
- Customer language, not company language
- Use cases before features
- Social proof: other customers with a similar problem
- Avoid technical or product jargon

**Attention triggers:** "customers like you were facing...", "now you can..."

---

### All-hands / Broad team

**What matters:** purpose, belonging, inspiring vision
**What does NOT matter:** operational detail level, decisions that are not yet mature

**Rules:**
- Emotional opening — a customer story or real impact moment
- Connect to "why we exist"
- Acknowledge completed work before talking about the future
- Room for questions — all-hands without Q&A is a monologue
- Honesty about what you don't know yet: "we don't have an answer for this yet, but we will by X"

**Attention triggers:** "here is a story of real impact...", "what you built made it possible to..."

## Quick Reference

| Audience | Goes first | Tone | Detail | Preferred output |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Exec/C-level | Recommendation | Direct | Minimum | exec-memo + 3 slides |
| Engineering | Trade-offs | Technical/honest | Maximum | deck-outline with tables |
| Squad PM | User's job | Collaborative | Medium | deck-outline |
| Customers | Benefit | Customer language | Use case | simple deck-outline |
| All-hands | Emotional story | Inspiring | Medium | deck-outline with visuals |
