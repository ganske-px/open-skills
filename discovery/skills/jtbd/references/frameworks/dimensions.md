# Job Dimensions: Functional, Emotional, and Social

Every job has three simultaneous dimensions. A product that addresses only the functional dimension
leaves room for competitors who better serve the emotional and social dimensions.

## Functional

**What it is:** the objective progress the customer needs to accomplish.
**Key question:** "What does the person need to DO or ACHIEVE?"

Generic examples:
- Approve a vendor within the agreed service window
- Identify the cause of a failed compliance check
- Initiate an engagement without outstanding risk flags

## Emotional

**What it is:** how the customer wants to FEEL during or after doing the job.
**Key question:** "How does the person want to feel? What do they want to avoid feeling?"

Common positive emotional states:
- Confident (in the decision being made)
- At ease (without worry about a future audit)
- In control (without depending on others to act)

Common negative emotional states to avoid:
- Anxious (about what might have been missed)
- Frustrated (with lack of visibility)
- Embarrassed (unable to explain a decision to a client)

## Social

**What it is:** how the customer wants to be PERCEIVED by others while doing the job.
**Key question:** "How does the person want to appear to colleagues, clients, or managers?"

Generic examples:
- Be seen as a thorough and reliable operator by the client
- Be recognized internally as the person who never lets a risk flag slip through
- Be perceived by the service provider as a fair and transparent organization

## Why Most PMs Ignore Emotional and Social

The functional dimension is the most visible — it shows up in tickets, NPS surveys, and feature requests. But competitive differentiation lives in the emotional and social dimensions:

- **Functional:** all competitors play here. Fast approvals are table stakes.
- **Emotional:** the operator who feels **confident** in the decision uses the product without escalating to their manager. The one who feels **anxious** creates workarounds and parallel processes — even when the product technically works.
- **Social:** the organization that can **demonstrate rigor** to its clients wins better contracts. The provider who is perceived as **trustworthy** receives more work.

**Concrete example:** Two operations managers use the same platform to approve service providers. Both achieve the same approval rate (functionally identical). But Manager A can export an auditable report for the client (social: "I look professional") and clearly sees why each decision was made (emotional: "I feel in control"). Manager B cannot — and loses contract renewals even with the same functional outcome.

## Prompts to Explore Beyond Functional

When the PM has extracted predominantly functional jobs, suggest exploring with these questions:

**For the emotional dimension:**
- "How does the person FEEL during this process? Confident? Anxious? Frustrated?"
- "If the functional job were perfectly served, would the person be satisfied? Or would they still have a concern?"
- "What would make this person sleep well at night after completing this task?"

**For the social dimension:**
- "Who else SEES the result of this job? A manager? A client? An auditor?"
- "How does this person want to be perceived by those around them while doing this?"
- "If they did this job perfectly, what would others say about them?"

**Signal that E/S is missing:**
- All jobs are about "doing X faster" or "getting Y without errors" → missing emotional
- No job mentions another person or external perception → missing social
- Jobs describe the what but not the emotional why → investigate

## How to Use the Three Dimensions

1. When extracting a job from an interview, identify all three dimensions explicitly or implicitly
2. If only the functional dimension appears, probe for emotional and social using the prompts above
3. In the job map, flag which dimensions are most at risk at each step
4. In the PR/FAQ, connect the proposed solution to all three dimensions — not just functional
5. In prioritization: functional is table stakes, emotional and social are differentiators
